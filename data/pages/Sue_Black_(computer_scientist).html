Susan Elizabeth Black OBE FBCS FRSA (born 1962) is a British computer scientist, academic and social entrepreneur.[2][3][1] She has been instrumental in saving Bletchley Park, the site of World War II codebreaking, with her Saving Bletchley Park campaign.[4][5]

Early life and education[edit]

Sue Black was born in 1962 in Fareham, Hampshire.[6] She left school and home at the earliest legal age, 16. She married at 20 and soon had three children.[7] By 25, she was a single mother living in a women's refuge. She took a maths access course at night school that led to enrolling in undergraduate degree.[8] Black graduated with a bachelor's degree in computing from London South Bank University[9] in 1993, and earned her PhD in software engineering[10] in 2001[11][12][13] for research on the ripple effect.[14]

Career and research[edit]

Black is a Senior Research Associate at University College London.[15] She was previously Head of the Department of Information and Software Systems at the University of Westminster. Black was the founding chair of the BCS Specialist Group BCSWomen[16] and remained in that position until 2008. She is an advocate of women in computing.[17]

Campaigns[edit]

Black ran a successful campaign over several years to secure funding for the restoration of Bletchley Park, the UK World War II centre for decrypting enemy messages.[4][18] In 2003, she started raising awareness of the site by creating a blog,[19] after visiting the site and noting the appalling condition of the buildings, seeing the Bombe machine being rebuilt, and hearing that over 10,000 people had worked there during the war. The photograph of one of the huts with a blue tarpaulin protecting the deteriorating structure helped galvanise interest and activism among concerned computer scientists. The initial outreach was followed up with an active Twitter presence that attracted even more followers and interested parties. Bletchley Park staff also engaged with other Web 2.0 (i.e. user-generated) technologies such as Facebook and Twitter.[20][21]

At the end of 2015, Black published a book about the process, Saving Bletchley Park,[4] initially funded via Unbound,[22] that became the fastest crowdfunded book of all time.[23]

everywoman says Black "embodies the traits of a modern leader", particularly in regard to social media use.[24] The BBC technology correspondent Rory Cellan-Jones spotted early on that Black was making sophisticated use of Twitter (and other platforms) in her campaigning.[25][26]

Black set up Techmums, reaching out to mothers who wanted to understand what their children were up to online. Techmums offers free training in digital security, social media, the programming language Python, and so on, with the aim of increasing the women's confidence, breaking down the digital divide, and helping them escape poverty. The pilot programme was held at Bishop Challoner Catholic Collegiate School in London Borough of Tower Hamlets.[8] Techmums describes itself as "an e-skills accredited program of short, hands-on workshops" with a supportive online community.[27]

Dr Black has appeared on BBC television, radio and in press articles.[18][28][29][30]

Awards and honours[edit]

In 2009, Black won the first John Ivinson Award[31] from the British Computer Society at the Royal Society in London. In 2011, Black won the PepsiCo Women's Inspiration Award.[32] In 2012, she was listed as one of Datamation's 10 Women in Tech Who Give Back.[33]

Black was also one of the 30 women identified in the British Computer Society's Women in IT Campaign in 2014, who were then featured in the e-book "Women in IT: Inspiring the next generation" produced by the BCS.[34]

In 2015, Black was identified as the 7th[35] Most Influential Women in UK IT 2015, by Computer Weekly.

She was appointed Officer of the Order of the British Empire (OBE) in the 2016 New Year Honours for services to technology.[36][37][38]

Black was awarded the 2017 Social Impact Abie Award from the AnitaB.org.[39][40]
