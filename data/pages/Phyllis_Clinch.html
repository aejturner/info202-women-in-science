Phyllis E. M. Clinch (12 September 1901 – 19 October 1984) was an Irish botanist most recognised for her work in the field of plant viruses.[1][2]

Clinch attained her undergraduate degree from University College Dublin in 1923 with a first class honours in botany and chemistry. She was then awarded a scholarship and continued to study under Joseph Doyle at University College Dublin obtaining a Masters in 1924.[1] She went on to study a PhD in plant physiology, specializing in the biochemistry of Coniferales.[1][3] From 1926-1928 she published multiple research articles on this topic.[4][5][6][7] After graduating with her PhD in 1928, she studied cytology at the Sorbonne with Alexandre Guilliermond.[3] She then became assistant to the professor of biology at University College, Galway.[1] In 1929 she became a Research Assistant for the investigation of Plant Virus Diseases,[8] Department of Plant Pathology, University College, Dublin.[1] In 1942, she served as an elected member of the Royal Dublin Society scientific council, and later she served as part of the council and then vice president.[3] In 1949 she transferred to the Botany Department, and the following year was appointed as a lecturer in Botany.[1] In 1961, she was appointed professor.[3] She retired in 1971.[3]

Notable work[edit]

Clinch is most recognised for her work on degenerative diseases in potato plants. She identified symptomless viruses and viruses that damaged potato stocks.[9][10][11] The Department of Agriculture used this knowledge to develop virus free stocks of potatoes.[1] She also carried out work on viruses affecting tomato and sugar beet crops.[12]

Awards[edit]

In 1943, Clinch was awarded an honorary Doctorate of Science, based on the strength of her publication record and service to the fields of virology and cytology.[2] Clinch was one of the first four women elected to membership of the Royal Irish Academy (RIA) in 1949.[1] She was awarded the Boyle Medal in 1961.[1] She was the first woman to have received this award.[13] Clinch's portrait appeared as one of twleve in an historic 2017 unveiling, when, for the first time in 230 years, women's portraits were hung in the Royal Irish Academy.[14]

Miscellanea[edit]

According to nephew Paul Clinch, in a brief biosketch by consulting company Accenture, generations of students referred to Clinch by the respectful nickname "Auntie Phyll."[15]
